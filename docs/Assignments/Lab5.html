Theory: The surface energy balance is a fundamental budget approach that describes the energy exchange at surface-atmosphere interfaces. It is based on the conservation equation of energy that says that energy can neither be created or destroyed by any ordinary means. The surface energy balance is essential to estimate evaporative water loss, cooling rates, growing conditions, frost damage, and many other important processes in natural and managed ecosystems.

The energy balance concept can be expressed as:

Energy Inputs = Energy Outputs + Energy Storage

The energy balance of an ecosystem is shown diagrammatically in the figure below. Positive values of the net radiation, Q* mean that the net flow of radiant energy is into the system (i.e., Q* is directed towards the surface); negative values mean the converse (i.e., Q* is directed away from the surface). This convention is used because Q* is regarded as an input to the system. Positive values of the sensible heat density, QH, the latent heat flux density, QE, and the soil heat flux density, QG, mean that energy is flowing out of the system (i.e., away from the surface); negative values mean the converse (i.e., towards the surface). This is because QH, QE and QG are regarded as outputs from the system.

Figure 8.1 Schematic of the energy balance of an ecosystem.

Positive values of the energy storage within the system, mean that the system is accumulating energy; negative values mean that the system is losing energy. Energy may be stored as sensible and latent heat in the canopy air QS, and chemical energy (photosynthesis) QP in the plants. Energy storage as a result of horizontal energy transport (advection) can occur in tall crops (e.g., orchards) and forests, particularly near their borders. The latent heat flux density QE is the energy used to evaporate water in the stomata (transpiration) and evaporate water in the soil near the soil surface (evaporation from the soil). The sum of transpiration and evaporation from the soil is evapotranspiration.

Net radiation is measured directly by a net radiometer which is usually located 1 to 3 metres above a crop (i.e., shorter vegetation) or 10 m above a forest (i.e., taller vegetation). It may be either a single-differential-thermopile net radiometer with upward- and downward-facing polyethylene domes that transmit shortwave and longwave radiation (as you saw at Totem field) or a four-way net radiometer consisting of upward- and downward-facing pyranometers and pyrgeometers (as shown in Lecture slides).

Soil heat flux density QG is measured by soil heat flux plates usually placed 2 to 5 cm below the soil surface. Thermometers and humidity sensors are used to determine the sensible and latent energy storage within a canopy Qs. Leaf chambers (cuvettes) can be used to measure net CO2 uptake by leaves or the eddy-covariance approach can be used to measure the CO2 uptake by the canopy as a whole. Biochemical energy storage QP can be calculated from the CO2 uptake rate. Canopy energy storage Qs is often neglected because it is generally small compared to Q*.

The latent and sensible heat flux densities are the most difficult terms in 8.1 to determine. Although there are a few ways to estimate these terms, today they are often measured directly using the eddy covariance technique.
