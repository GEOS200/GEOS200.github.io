1 Radiative Properties of Natural Surfaces

Recall from from Lab 1: sets of pyranometers and pyrgeometers, one of each pointing upward toward the sky and downward toward the ground, are used to measure both incoming and outgoing short-wave (\(SW\)) and long-wave (\(LW\)) radiation respectively. We differentiate \(SW\) and \(LW\) by based on wavelength (\(\lambda\)); the distinction is relative and will may see some variability across the literature, but we typically use 3 \(\mu m\) as the cutoff Figure 1.

  • \(SW\) = $< 3 m
  • \(LW\) = $>= 3 m

Net Radiation

These four components together constitute Net All-Wave Radiation (\(R_n\)), which is defined the sum of all radiation components Equation 1. \[ R_n = (SW \downarrow - SW\uparrow) + (LW\downarrow - LW\uparrow) \tag{1}\]

Short-wave (aka solar) radiation is emitted from the sun. Climate of an ecosystem is largely determined by the amount of incoming short wave radiation (\(SW \downarrow\)) that reaches the ecosystem and the proportion of that radiation that is absorbed (\(a\)) by the plant, soil and water surfaces versus the portion that is reflected (\(\alpha\)) by the surface.

  • When \(SW \downarrow\) that is absorbed by the earth’s surface, it is re-emitted by the surface as long-wave radiation (\(LW \uparrow\)), aka (aka thermal radiation). Figure 1 highlights the difference between short-wave radiation (emitted by the sun) and long-wave radiation (emitted by the earth).

A portion of \(SW \downarrow\) is reflected by the earths surface back towards the sky, this is known as outgoing, or reflected short-wave radiation (\(SW \uparrow\)).

Net Radiation can describe how much energy is entering or leaving a given area. Incoming short-wave \((\downarrow SW)\) is measured by a pyranometer pointing up to the sky and short-wave radiation outgoing (reflected) from the underlying surface \((\uparrow SW)\) is measured by a pyranometer pointed at the ground. Likewise, incoming long-wave radiation \((\downarrow LW)\) is measured by a pyrgeometer pointing up to the sky and outgoing (emitted) long-wave radiation \((\uparrow LW)\) is measured by a pyrgeometer pointing at the ground. Knowing the four radiation components, we can then calculate net radiation \((R_n)\). Net radiation can tell us how much energy is entering (positive) or leaving (negative) the site at any given time. If net \((R_n)\) is a large positive number, that indicates significant energy input, which would cause the land surface at the site to warm up. Conversely, if \((R_n)\) is negative, that means the site is loosing energy (cooling off).

Tropical climates are hot because they receive ample \(SW \downarrow\) all year long. Polar climates are cold because they receive very little \(SW \downarrow\) over the course of a year, and much of the \(SW \downarrow\) they do receive is reflected as \(SW \uparrow\) by snowy surfaces.

Theory

All radiation striking a surface or an object must be either absorbed, transmitted, or reflected:

\[ a_\lambda + \tau_\lambda + \alpha_\lambda = 1 \tag{2}\]

The coefficients of absorption (absorptivity \(a_\lambda\)), transmission (transmissivity \(\tau_\lambda\)), and reflection (reflectivity \(\alpha_\lambda\)) are not the same for all kinds of radiation. Their values depend on the wavelength \(\lambda\) of the incident radiation (e.g., difference between wavelengths from a fluorescent and incandescent lamp). When talking specifically about \(\alpha_{SW}\) (reflected short wave radiation), we can also define the term albedo, the coefficient of reflection for short wave radiation:

\[ albedo = \alpha_{SW} = \frac{\uparrow SW}{\downarrow SW} \tag{3}\]

Given the definition for albedo (\(\alpha_{SW}\)), we can also re-define \(SW\uparrow = \alpha_{SW} SW\uparrow\) and therefore, rewrite Equation 1 as:

\[ R_n = (1-\alpha_{SW})SW\downarrow + (LW\downarrow - LW\uparrow) \tag{4}\]

  • Note: This definition works because the earth’s surface is not a source of short-wave radiation; it can reflect \(SW\), but it does not emit \(SW\).

To describe a radiation source, we can plot its spectrum. Spectra for solar radiation and radiation from a fluorescent lamp and an incandescent tungsten filament lamp are shown in Figure 2. In this diagram, the spectra have been adjusted to equal peak heights.

Plant leaves have interesting radiative properties that are part of the plants’ adaptation to their environments. The coefficients of reflection, transmission and absorption are strongly dependent on wavelength as shown by a typical example in Figure 3. Energy is strongly absorbed in the visible region where radiation is active in photosynthesis. In the near infrared region (NIR) radiation is more strongly reflected and transmitted. The reflectivity and absorptivity of soils and animal coats are also dependent on wavelength.

Units of wavelength are often given in micrometers (1 \(\mu m\) = 10-6 m). Radiation energy flux density can be measured by sensors that produce electrical signals. In this laboratory the selenium photocell produces a voltage that is proportional to the flux of shortwave radiation striking the sensor. The sensor is not sensitive to longwave radiation. The selenium photocell has its maximum (peak) response at \(\lambda\) = 0.5 \(\mu m\).

2 Earth’s Radiation Budget

This exercise is designed to acquaint you with the component fluxes of a radiation budget.

The following simple computations help to demonstrate the radiation relationships existing between the Sun (the Earth’s only source of energy) and the Earth and the Earth and Space. They explain the sizes of the radiation streams between these bodies and so doing illustrate the concept of a radiation budget.

RECALL:

Flux = Energy flow, Watts or W [i.e., Joules per second]

Flux Density = Energy flow divided by area (W/m2).

The components of Equation 1: \(R_n = (SW \downarrow - SW\uparrow) + (LW\downarrow - LW\uparrow)\)

  • \(R_n\) is the net all-wave radiative flux density.

  • \(SW \downarrow\) is the incoming short-wave radiation flux density (= short-wave irradiance).

  • \(SW\uparrow\) is the short-wave radiation reflected by the surface (= short-wave reflectance), i.e., \(SW\uparrow = \alpha_{SW} SW\downarrow\), where \(\alpha_SW\) is the surface albedo.

  • \(LW\downarrow\) is the long-wave (= thermal infrared) radiative flux density reaching the surface from the atmosphere or other objects.

  • \(LW\uparrow\) is the long-wave radiative flux density emitted by the surface to the atmosphere.

  • For a blackbody, emission is given by the Stefan-Boltzman Law, where \(\sigma\) is the Stefan-Boltzman constant and T0 is the surface temperature in Kelvin. \[ LW\uparrow = \sigma T^4 \tag{5}\]

  • For a grey body, it is necessary to add the surface emissivity \(\epsilon\) and a reflection term:

\[ LW\uparrow = \epsilon\sigma T^4 + (1-\epsilon) LW\downarrow \tag{6}\]

The all-wave budget can also be considered to consist of a net short-wave budget, \(SW* = SW\downarrow - SW\uparrow\) and a long-wave budget \(SW* = SW\downarrow - SW\uparrow\) , i.e. \(R_n = SW* + LW*\). The sign convention adopted here is that individual fluxes have no sign (i.e., always positive) but net fluxes (those with an asterisk) are positive if they are a net energy gain for the surface, and negative if they are a net energy loss.
