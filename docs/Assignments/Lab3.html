Lab 3: Atmospheric Humidity

This assignment is worth 11 points.

Objectives

This laboratory is intended to give you some familiarity with these various expressions for atmospheric water content. We compare measurements of vapor density, vapor pressure, relative humidity, dew point temperature and vapor density deficit inside the classroom with measurements outdoors.

  • Differentiate between the ways moisture is represented in the atmosphere, specifically, dew point temperature, absolute humidity, vapor pressure, saturation vapor pressure, relative humidity and vapor pressure deficit.
  • Using equations, describe the relationship between different measures of atmospheric humidity

1 Theory

Water vapor constitutes a fairly small part of our atmosphere Table 2. However, it plays a disproportionately important role in the Earth’s climate system in your day to day life! We use the term humidity to describe the amount of water vapor present in a given parcel of air.

  • The rate of evaporation of water from a surface depends on atmospheric humidity. Thus the rate at which a soil dries and vegetation consumes water, depends in part on the atmospheric humidity.

  • Additionally, evaporation from the lungs and of sweat from the skin is an important mechanism for maintaining a consistent body temperature in mammals and birds. The efficiency of the evaporative cooling depends on evaporation rate which, in turn, depends partly on the atmospheric humidity.

The Ideal Gas Law

You may recall from a high school chemistry class, the Ideal Gas Law (Equation 1) can be used to describe the state of some a gas as a function of pressure (\(P\)), volume (\(V\)), temperature (\(T\)), and the amount of the gas (\(n\)) in moles; with \(R\) being the ideal gas constant(see Table 1). The amount in moles of a given gas can be defined as \(n = \frac{m}{M}\), where \(m\) is the mass of the substance (in kg or g) and \(M\) is the Molar mass (in kg mol-1 or g mol-1) respectively. The molar mass of water (in g mol-1) is listed in Table 1. The Ideal Gas Law generally applies to water vapor as it does other gasses and can be used to help explain some important relationships.

\[ PV=nRT \tag{1}\]

Two key metrics we use to describe atmospheric moisture in meteorology are:

  1. Vapor density: \(\rho_v\)
  • This metric quantifies the density of water vapor within a parcel of air and is typically given in g m-3
    • It describes mass of H20 vapor (in grams) per unit volume (cubic meter) of air, i.e. \(\frac{m_{H2O}}{V}\).
  1. Vapor pressure: \(P_v\)
  • This metric quantifies the partial pressure exerted by the water vapor in a parcel of air and is typically given in kPa.
    • The pressure exerted by the water vapor in the atmosphere, is only a small portion of the total pressure exerted by the atmosphere. The pressure of the various gasses is proportional composition of gasses in the atmosphere (see Table 2)

We can redefine the ideal gas law in terms of \(\rho_v^*\) and \(P_v^*\) as Equation 2 to make our calculations easier:

\[ \rho_v = \frac{\rho_vM}{RT} \tag{2}\]

Saturation

Under the pressures and temperature conditions experienced on Earth the atmosphere can only hold a small amount of water vapor before it becomes saturated. We add an asterisk to \(\rho_v^*\) and \(P_v^*\) to denote saturation vapor density and saturation vapor pressure respectively. Once the atmosphere reaches saturation point, H2O vapor in the air will begin to condense into its liquid form.

Determining exact value of \(\rho_v^*\) and \(P_v^*\) for a specific value of \(T\) isn’t a straightforward calculation. This is because we need to consider the latent heat of vaporization (recall this term for lecture). When water changes phase, it adds (condensation/freezing) or removes (evaporation/melting) a massive amount of energy from its surrounding environment. Calculating \(\rho_v^*\) and \(P_v^*\) exactly involves using some calculus to evaluate a differential equation known as the Clausius–Clapeyron relation. But don’t worry, we’re not going to make you do any calculus here. Empirically derived relationships have been derived to help simplify the calculations. On such relationship, is known as the Buck equation (Equation 3). Note: this empirical equation requires \(T\) to be in \(^\circ C\), instead of \(K\), which is different from most from many others. The ideal gas law Equation 1 and it’s variant Equation 2 require temperature to be in K.

\[ P_v^*(T)=0.61121e^{(18.678-\frac{T}{234.5})(\frac{T}{257.14+T})} \tag{3}\]

By evaluating Equation 3, we can come up with an accurate approximation for \(P_v^*\) to a high degree of accuracy (\(/pm\) 0.08%) within the ranges of temperatures and pressures typically observed on Earths surface. We can then plug the result into Equation 2 to solve for \(\rho_v\). Remembering to convert our temperature values to K in order to evaluate this variant of the ideal gas law. The R code shown in Example 1 shows how you could perform these calculations in programmatically.

Example 1  

# Functions are pieces of code that can be defined once an run numerous times
# They make performing calculations that need to be repeated much easier
# Functions may take one or more inputs in parentheses ()
# They and will evaluate the instructions contained between the curly brackets {}
# The "return()" command can optionally be used to return one or more outputs

Buck_Equation <- function(T){
    # This function evaluates the Buck Equation 
    # It take an air temperature in Celsius as input
    # It outputs (returns) the saturation vapor pressure in kPa
    P_v <- 0.61121*exp((18.678-T/234.5)*(T/(257.14+T)))
    return(P_v)
}

Ideal_Gas_Law_Vapor_Density <- function(T,P_v){
    # This function evaluates the Ideal Gas Law for water vapor
    # It take an air temperature in Celsius and vapor pressure in kPa as inputs. 
    # It first converts the temperature to Kelvin 
    # Then it evaluates the equation and returns the vapor density in g m-3

    # Constants
    R <- 8.31446261815324-3 # Ideal Gas Constant in kPa m$^3$ g$^{-1}$ K$^{-1}$
    M <- 18.01528 # Molar mass of H20 in g m-3

    # Converts temperature to Kelvin
    T_k <- T + 273.15

    rho_v = P_v*M/(R*T_k)
    return (rho_v)
}

Measuring Atmospheric Humidity

The most common type of instrument used to measure humidity is the psychrometer (Figure 3). This instrument consists simply of a pair of thermometers, one of which has a wetted wick enclosing the sensor. The wet-bulb is cooled by evaporation to a temperature that depends on the atmospheric vapor density.

The water vapor density \(\rho_v\) (aka. absolute humidity) can be calculated from measurements of wet-bulb temperature (\(T_w\)) and dry-bulb temperature (\(T\)) using this relationship:

\[ \rho_v = \rho^*_{vw}(T_w) - \gamma(T-T_w) \tag{4}\]

Other ways of expressing the amount of moisture are used and each has particular usefulness in certain applications. The total amount of water that the atmosphere can hold without condensation depends only on temperature. This quantity, called the saturation vapor density \(\rho^*_v(T)\), is given for a range of temperatures in Figure 2. The ratio of actual vapor density to the maximum possible (i.e. the saturation vapor density) when expressed as a percentage is called the relative humidity:

\[ RH = \frac{\rho_v}{\rho^*_v(T)} \tag{5}\]

For some calculations of evaporation rate, the difference between the maximum and the actual vapor density is useful. This is called the vapor density deficit:

\[ VDD = \rho^*_v(T) - \rho_v \tag{6}\]

We may express the water vapor content of air by finding the temperature to which a parcel of air could be cooled at constant atmospheric pressure before condensation commences. This temperature is called the dew point temperature \(T_d\). Note: that for a saturated parcel the dewpoint temperature is the temperature of the parcel. For any water vapor density, \(T_d\) can be found by using Figure 2 in reverse. For example, air with a vapor density of 7.8 g m-3 has a \(T_d\) of 7°C.

Water vapor content of the air can be expressed in terms of the partial pressure exerted by the water vapor, or water vapor pressure, \(e\). This is related to vapor density by the gas law as follows:

\[ e = \rho_v R_v T \tag{7}\]

Where R_v is the gas constant for water vapor (see Table 1) and \(T\) is the absolute temperature (K). Using units of g m-3 for \(\rho_v(T)\) results in units of kPa (kilopascals) for \(e\).

Saturation vapor pressure, \(e*(T)\): Consider a parcel at temperature \(T\). Unless the parcel is currently saturated, the parcel could hold more water vapor than it currently does. The partial pressure exerted by the water vapor is given by \(e\), whereas the partial pressure that would be exerted by the water vapor if the parcel were saturated, is \(e*\), which is a function of temperature: i.e., \(e*(T)\). (Units: kPa).

Vapor pressure deficit, VPD: Vapor pressure deficit represents the difference between the actual and saturation vapor pressures of a parcel at temperature T:

\[ VPD = e*(T) - e \tag{8}\]

  • Note: that the smaller the VPD, the closer the parcel is to saturation. (Units: kPa)

3 Experimental procedure

This is the experimental procedure, describing how we obtain measurements of the water vapor content of the air.

Psychrometer

To make measurements of water vapor content of the air, you will use the Assmann aspirated psychrometer. Examine the psychrometer and notice the wick on the wet-bulb thermometer. Use distilled water to wet the wick thoroughly. Wind up the clockwork ventilation fan. Take the instrument outdoors and measure the wet- and dry bulb temperatures. Read measurements every 15 s for 3 or 4 minutes until the thermometer reaches equilibrium. Use the equilibrium values for further calculations.

Return to the laboratory, re-wet the wick and re-wind the psychrometer. Repeat the measurements of wet- and dry-bulb temperature. The water vapor density in the air in the classroom and outdoors can now be calculated (see 5.4, Question 1).
