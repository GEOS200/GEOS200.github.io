1 Measuring Atmospheric Humidity

This laboratory is intended to give you some familiarity with these various expressions for atmospheric water content. We compare measurements of vapor density, vapor pressure, relative humidity, dew point temperature and vapor density deficit inside the classroom with measurements outdoors.

Water vapor constitutes only a very small part of our atmosphere, yet it is extremely important. The rate of evaporation of water from a surface depends on atmospheric humidity. Thus the rate at which a field soil dries or the rate at which a crop or forest consumes water which must be replaced by precipitation or irrigation, depends in part on the atmospheric humidity. Evaporation from the lungs and of perspiration from the skin is an important mechanism in the maintenance of constant internal temperature in mammals and birds. The efficiency of the evaporative cooling depends on evaporation rate which, in turn, depends partly on the atmospheric humidity.

Theory

The most common type of instrument used to measure humidity is the psychrometer. This consists simply of a pair of thermometers, one of which has a wetted wick enclosing the sensor. The wet-bulb is cooled by evaporation to a temperature that depends on the atmospheric vapor density.

The water vapor density \(\rho_v\) (= absolute humidity) can be calculated from measurements of wet- and dry-bulb temperatures using the relationship:

\[ \rho_v = \rho^*_{vw}(T_w) - \gamma(T-T_w) \tag{1}\]

These and other symbols in this exercise are defined in Table 5.1. The density of water vapor in the atmosphere \(\rho_v(T)\), is the basic measure of atmospheric moisture. It is the mass of water vapor in unit volume of air (Units: kg m-3 but often given in g m-3 to make more sensibly sized numbers).

Other ways of expressing the amount of moisture are used and each has particular usefulness in certain applications. The total amount of water that the atmosphere can hold without condensation depends only on temperature. This quantity, called the saturation vapor density ^*_v(T), is given for a range of temperatures in Table 3. The ratio of actual vapor density to the maximum possible (i.e. the saturation vapor density) when expressed as a percentage is called the relative humidity:

\[ RH = \frac{\rho_v}{\rho^*_v(T)} \tag{2}\]

For some calculations of evaporation rate, the difference between the maximum and the actual vapor density is useful. This is called the vapor density deficit:

\[ VDD = \rho^*_v(T) - \rho_v \tag{3}\]

We may express the water vapor content of air by finding the temperature to which a parcel of air could be cooled at constant atmospheric pressure before condensation commences. This temperature is called the dew point temperature \(T_d\). Note: that for a saturated parcel the dewpoint temperature is the temperature of the parcel. For any water vapor density, \(T_d\) can be found by using Table 3 in reverse. For example, air with a vapor density of 7.8 g m-3 has a \(T_d\) of 7°C.

Water vapor content of the air can be expressed in terms of the partial pressure exerted by the water vapor, or water vapor pressure, \(e\). This is related to vapor density by the gas law as follows:

\[ e = \rho_v R_v T \tag{4}\]

Where R_v is the gas constant for water vapor (4.62 ×10-4 kPa m3 g-1 K-1) and T is the absolute temperature (K). Using units of g m-3 for \(\rho_v(T)\) results in units of kPa (kilopascals) for \(e\).

Saturation vapor pressure, \(e*(T)\): Consider a parcel at temperature \(T\). Unless the parcel is currently saturated, the parcel could hold more water vapor than it currently does. The partial pressure exerted by the water vapor is given by \(e\), whereas the partial pressure that would be exerted by the water vapor if the parcel were saturated, is e*, which is a function of temperature: i.e., \(e*(T)\). (Units: kPa).

Vapor pressure deficit, VPD: Vapor pressure deficit represents the difference between the actual and saturation vapor pressures of a parcel at temperature T:

\[ VPD = e*(T) - e \tag{5}\]

  • Note: that the smaller the VPD, the closer the parcel is to saturation. (Units: kPa)

2 Experimental procedure

This is the experimental procedure, describing how we obtain measurements of the water vapor content of the air.

Psychrometer

To make measurements of water vapor content of the air, you will use the Assmann aspirated psychrometer. Examine the psychrometer and notice the wick on the wet-bulb thermometer. Use distilled water to wet the wick thoroughly. Wind up the clockwork ventilation fan. Take the instrument outdoors and measure the wet- and dry bulb temperatures. Read measurements every 15 s for 3 or 4 minutes until the thermometer reaches equilibrium. Use the equilibrium values for further calculations.

Return to the laboratory, re-wet the wick and re-wind the psychrometer. Repeat the measurements of wet- and dry-bulb temperature. The water vapor density in the air in the classroom and outdoors can now be calculated (see 5.4, Question 1).
