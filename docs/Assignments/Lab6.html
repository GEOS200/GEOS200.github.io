1 Theory

The net exchange of carbon dioxide (CO2) between terrestrial ecosystems and the atmosphere, called net ecosystem exchange NEE, is the difference between ecosystem respiration Re and gross ecosystem photosynthesis GEP, i.e., (8.1) When NEE is positive (i.e., Re > GEP), the atmosphere gains CO2 and when it’s negative (Re < GEP) the ecosystem gains CO2. Thus, net ecosystem productivity NEP is (8.2) Soil respiration (Rs), which can account for as much as 80% of Re, results in the efflux of CO2 from the soil. Rs is composed of the CO2 produced by microbial decomposition of soil organic matter (SOM) (heterotrophic respiration, Rsh) and by root respiration (autotrophic respiration, Rsa), i.e., (8.3) The autotrophic respiration of stems, branches and leaves accounts for the remainder of Re.

Temperature T, followed by soil moisture, is the most important environmental factor affecting Rs (and all its components). It increases exponentially with T, which is often expressed as (8.4) where Rref is Rs at a reference temperature Tref, usually 10 °C, and Q10 is the relative increase in Rs (or Re) for a 10 K increase in T. Q10 is about 2 in many biological processes but is frequently found to be as much as 3 for Re and Rs. This sensitivity, which is particularly important in predicting the effects of global warming, depends on the quality (i.e., labile or recalcitrant) and quantity of substrate and thus productivity and soil temperature and moisture. The soil is the largest terrestrial carbon pool. It is particularly vulnerable to warming and its potential loss may result in a `positive feedback’ effect, i.e., increased atmospheric CO2, which could lead to further global warming as a result of the greenhouse effect (Cox et al. 2000). In addition to T and soil moisture, Rsh depends on SOM content and its recalcitrance.

A widely used method of measuring Rs is the closed or static chamber method. In this method, the rate of increase in CO2 concentration in a chamber placed on the soil surface is used to determine the soil CO2 efflux (Livingston and Hutchinson, 1995). This is a good measure of Rs because very little of the respired CO2 accumulates in the soil. The CO2 concentration in the chamber is measured with an infrared gas analyzer (IRGA). If the chamber is kept on the soil for less than 2 minutes, r is almost constant. This means the rise in chamber CO2 concentration does not significantly suppress CO2 efflux. Because CO2 concentration changes during the measurement, the measurement apparatus is described as a non-steady-state system.
