Kirchhoff’s Law

Emissivity = Absorptivity

\[ a_{\lambda} = \epsilon_{\lambda} \qquad(1)\]

Kirchhoff’s law

  • Assuming no transmission the absorptivity of a body (aλ) equals its emissivity (ελ)
  • A good absorber is a good emitter – also true that if a surface is a poor absorber it also doesn’t emit that kind of radiation.

Another Law to remember! It tells us that a particular wavelength, objects that absorb radiation well at that wavelength, are equally good emitters of radiation at that wavelength.

Kirchhoff’s law.

  • Be sure to note that Kirchhoff’s law only applies if the wavelength considered is the same – do not mix them together.
  • Since no bodies in the Earth-Atmosphere system are hot enough to emit solar radiation Kirchhoff’s law only has relevance to long-wave exchange in climatology.

The atmosphere is a selective absorber in the Long-wave.

The most significant absorbing gases are water vapour (the most important), CO2, O3, O2, N2O, and CH4 Aerosols (particles) and liquid water in cloud if present will be dominant.

Atmospheric absorption and emission

Kirchhoff’s Law tells us that since atmospheric absorption is concentrated at these wavelengths so is atmospheric emission. Equally, at wavelengths where the atmosphere is poor at absorbing it is poor at emitting.

Practical implications! If we can reduce Long-wave loss then plants would be warmer, right?

‘Greenhouse’ is not a good analogy because warming in greenhouses largely due to reduction in convective heat transfer by shelter, and to a lesser extent due to increased downward Long-wave emission from the glass. In the Atmosphere convection is not constrained

View factors

  • The view of the sky from an object (sky view factor, sky) is
  • significant in quantifying Long-wave exchange at night. This is
  • because the sky is usually ‘cold’ and an effective heat sink
  • Refers to the fraction of radiation leaving one object that is intercepted by another object
  • Or the fraction of one object’s view (usually hemispherical) that is occupied by another object

The more open a site is (the more of the sky hemisphere is visible from a point at the surface) has an impact on the amount of Long-wave radiation that can be emitted to atmosphere (and hence cool the surface)

Sky view factor, sky applications

A clump of trees can keep livestock warm at night by reducing the animals’ sky view factor

Take home points

  • Long-wave radiation is emitted from Atmosphere (gases, aerosols, droplets) and surface at ambient temperatures.
  • Kirchhoff’s Law states that for a certain wavelength the absorptivity aλ equals emissivity ελ.
  • Absorptivities and emissivities of most natural surface materials are high in the Long-wave, but not for gases.
  • The ‘atmospheric window’ is a region in the Long-wave where few gases interfere - often transparent.
