Radiation is emitted from all matter

We have all seen thermal infra-red imagery! ALL OBJECTS above absolute zero tempertaure emit radiation. The amount of radiation, and the wavelengths of that radiation are a function primarily of TEMPERATURE.

Characteristics of radiation

  • It is the emission of energy as electromagnetic waves or as moving particles.
  • Can travel in a vacuum (space).
  • Travels in straight lines.
  • Travels at c = 3 x 108 m s-1 (speed of light) - e.g. it takes 8 min 20 s for radiation to travel from the Sun to the Earth.

light behaves as a wave & as a particle

The electromagnetic spectrum

The radaition coming from the sun (solar radiation) is part of spectrum of electromagnetic radiation that includes X-rays, microwaves, radio waves etc. Our eyes are adapted to see a large portion of that solar radiation which we call “visible” light. Each colour that we see is associated with a particular wavelength of solar radiation. Green light for example has a wavelength of approximately 0.5 micrometers (millions of a meter).

Radiant flux density

This a reminder from the previous lecture. We will use the term Wm-2 extensively through this class. It denotes the amount of energy (in this case radiative energy) passing through a square meter every second.

Dual properties of radiation

    1. Wave-like property
  • Wavelength λ (in m) – distance between crests
  • Frequency ν (in s-1) where c is the speed of light, i.e., speed of the wave.
    1. Particle-like property
  • Energy emitted in discrete packets (photons or quanta)
  • Energy of a photon e = hν, where h (Planck’s constant) = 6.63 x 10-34 J s

Physicists tend to think of the behaviour of waves in two different ways. The first is to describe them as have a frequency and wavelength (just like waves in the ocean). The other is to view radiation of being like Particles, that is being discrete packets of energy or photons.

Wien’s displacement law

States that the wavelength of maximum emission from an object* is inversely proportional to its absolute temperature T (in K) of its surface:

  • As T increases, λmax decreases (e.g. iron heated changes from dull red and to bright red – orange – yellow – white ‘hot’). Remember that the wavelength of red is longer than that of yellow.
  • This relation can be used to determine the T of an emitting object without having to measure the actual flux density of emitted radiation.

Stefan-Boltzmann law - applications

An infrared thermometer uses the Stefan-Boltzmann law to measure the surface temperature of objects
