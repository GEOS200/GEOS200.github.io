Solar constant I0

The Nimbus satellite platforms hosted the first instruments measuring the solar constant and Earth’s radiation budget directly from space (NASA).

The Solar constant I0 is the solar radiant flux density (irradiance) at the top of the atmosphere normal to the solar beam an at Earth’s mean distance from Sun. Present observations suggest I0 ≈ 1366 W m-2.

The “solar constant represents about the upper limit for solar energy in the Earth-atmosphere system. Imagine astronauts in the space station doing a space walk where they take a a board that is 1 meter by one meter and then measuring how much radiation falls on it when they point it directly at the sun. NOTE: the word “constant” here is a bit misleading. Of courses the distance from the earth varies because of the elliptical orbit of the earth around the sun. The differences are small however.

Attenuation of solar radiation in the atmosphere

  • On its path from the top of the atmosphere to the ground, solar radiation is:
  • reflected back to space by clouds
  • scattered by aerosols and droplets
  • absorbed by certain gases, droplets and aerosols

Photo: A. Christen

Reflection

  • Cloud tops are most significant. Clouds reflect 40 to 80% of the short-wave radiation incident upon them, the average is 55%*.
    • We call this the cloud albedo, hence the ratio of the amount of solar (short-wave) radiation reflected by a body to the amount incident upon it.

Reflection from clouds is really important! This is why the earth looks so bright from space. Clouds viewed from the sides or above are also very white (an indicator that they are reflecting all wavelengths of visible light).

Absorption in the atmosphere

  • The atmosphere is a selective absorber of solar radiation.
  • The most significant absorbing substances are:
  • Water vapour: important for λ > 0.8 μm, absorption bands are at 1.1, 1.45, 1.95 μm
  • O2 and O3: important for λ < 0.3 μm
  • CO2: important for λ > 2.5 μm
  • The atmosphere is quite transparent near λmax = 0.48 μm where the intensity of solar radiation is greatest.

As well as reflection of radiation from clouds, the atmosphere also ABSORBS solar radiation.

See Video: The “Atmospheric Window” is an important concept. You need to understand it. Simply put, the window part is analogy. It implies that in the 8-11 micrometer longwave (terrestrial) part of the radiation spectrum where there are no gases that absorb outgoing longwave radiation from the earth/atmosphere. Therefore this radiation is free to escape to space through these wavelengths. If this wasn’t the case, then the earth would continue to heat up over time. The role of “greenhouse” gases in the atmosphere is of course part of this story

Scattering

increasing particle size

Illustration: Georgia State University.

  • Scattering is a process like reflection in which incident light is diffused in all directions by the smallest gaseous molecules and aerosols (e.g. smoke). Scattering creates the blue sky color and red sunsets. If particles are larger (e.g. water droplets) the sky is hazy and whiter.
  • Together these processes reduce the solar radiation getting to the ground and diffuse the beam so that it arrives from all parts of the sky hemisphere.

Scattering is usually divided into two types: Rayleigh scattering is caused by gas molecules and tends to scatter in all directions with the shortest wavelengths scattered most (hence blue light giving rise to the blue sky) Mie scattering is associated with much large solid or liquid particles (water droplets, smoke, sea salt haze) scatter all wavelength equally and mostly in a forward direction. Because all wavelengths are scattered equally the result is a “white” appearance to the sky.

Transmission

  • Typical fractions of the solar getting through to the ground are:
  • “Clean” atmosphere τ = 0.65-0.75
  • “Dirty” atmosphere τ = 0.45-0.55
  • In Vancouver, a typical value is τ = 0.70

If light is not reflected, absorbed or scattered it must be transmitted to the ground. The Greek letter tau is used to denote the proportion of light reaching the ground compared to that at the outer edge of the atmosphere. In Vancouver we typically have about 70% of solar radiation at the outer edge of the atmosphere reaching the ground.

Cosine law of illumination

  • The cosine law of illumination describes how the incident radiant flux density (irradiance) of a beam decreases as the beam’s angle varies from overhead (normal) to a more slanted path relative to the surface.
  • Notice that the same energy is spread over a larger surface area when a beam is tilted away from overhead (or the surface is tilted)

This is an important concept! Try it yourself at home with a flashlight. You can also shine it on a globe (or ball) to see the impact on Latitude (ie.e why the poles receive less radiation than the tropics.

Irradiance

  • Shortwave irradiance (K↓) is the integration of all radiative fluxes coming from the upper hemisphere per unit area
  • (and is hence a radiative flux density in W m-2).

Direct and diffuse irradiance

  • Two types of solar radiation arrive at the Earth’s surface:
  • Direct (S): comes directly in parallel rays from Sun.
  • Diffuse (D): after scattering and reflection by the Earth’s atmosphere.

When you look at the horizon on a sunny day you still see BLUE – that is short wavelength visible radiation being scattered toward you (Rayleigh scattering)

Thermopile

Solar radiation (K↓ = S + D) is measured by a pyranometer. It has glass domes (transmit only short-wave radiation, prevent conduction / convection) and a thermopile (measures the temperature rise of the absorbing black surface).

Pyranometer

Two glass domes

Photos: A. Christen

Inside the double glass dome (to prevent convection and conduction) there is a black absorber disk (the sensing element), which is made of a black carbon with a high absorptivity. The temperature rise of the sensing element due to short-wave irradiance is measured relatively to the (more constant) body temperature of the pyranometer. A thermopile (array of thermocouples) measures the temperature difference, which creates a voltage and then is measured by the data logger. The black carbon would be also sensitive to long-wave radiation, but the glass dome filters out the long-wave emission. A double glass dome reduces heating of the inner dome (long-wave emission of the dome).

Direct and diffuse radiation - shade ring

The shade ring follows the solar path and eliminates S so only D is measured by the pyranometer. Together with a non-shaded pyranometer this allows us to separate S and D.

Take home points

  • Short-wave radiation = solar radiation.
  • What is the meaning of the solar constant I0?
  • On its way through the atmosphere solar radiation is reflected, scattered, and absorbed by certain gas molecules, droplets and particles.
  • The magnitude of the short-wave irradiance at the ground is mainly determined by the angle of the beam (cosine law of illumination).
  • We distinguish between direct and diffuse shortwave irradiance.
