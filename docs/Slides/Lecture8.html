Combining the equations…

Q* is the main energy source driving near-surface climates. It can be positive (usually by day) or negative (usually at night).

  •         Q* = K* + L*
  •               = K↓(1 - α) + εs L↓ - εs σTs4

Reflected

  •        K* = K↓ - K↑           
  •                     = K↓(1 - α)
  •         L* = L↓ - L↑
  •              = L↓ - [εsσTs4  + L↓(1 - εs )]

If Q* is negative it means the surface is losing energy. This is what happens at night when the surface is emitting longwave radiation but there is no incoming shortwave radiation.

Summary of our interpretation

  • There is a strong correlation between Q* and K↓, i.e. the daily Q* variation is mainly driven by the solar cycle.
  • Daytime shortwave gain (K) is only partly offset by more constant net loss of longwave radiation (L) emission from warmer surface exceeds input from sky.
  • At night, longwave loss is unopposed due to absence of shortwave input.

Nighttime cooling - Frost damage risk!

  • Nighttime surface cooling is only controlled by longwave radiation:
  • The net long-wave L* is usually negative (all-day). Clouds have a large effect on L↓ and the nights with lowest L↓ (and hence most negative L*) are those with cloudless skies.

Clouds play a huge role in absorbing and re-radiating downward nighttime longwave radiation from surfaces. Cloudy conditions therefore tend to produce warmer nights.

View factor

  • Refers to the fraction of radiation leaving one object that is intercepted by another object
  • Or the fraction of one object’s view (usually hemispherical) that is occupied by another object
  • The view of the sky from an object (sky view factor, ψsky) is significant in quantifying long-wave exchange at night. This is because the sky is usually ‘cold’ and an effective heat sink.

Take home points

  • Distinguish between net shortwave (K), net longwave (L) and net all-wave radiation (Q*).
  • The magnitude of Q* is controlled by surface properties (albedo, surface temperature, emissivity)
  • The magnitude of Q* is further controlled by the surrounding 3D surface. The term sky view factor is an important parameter that controls the magnitude of L* during night. Why?
  • Surface properties and geometry can be modified to control surface climates.
