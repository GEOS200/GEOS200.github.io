Energy Transfer

Laminar vs. Turbulent Flow

Laminar flow: parallel streamlines

  • Mixing is inefficient, only occurs by diffusion

Turbulent flow: irregular streamlines

  • Mixing is very efficient and occurs mainly by convection

Turbulence

Turbulence is a feature of flows, not fluids.

  • Turbulent flows are very efficient in equalizing temperature and concentration gradients:

    • In the Atmosphere, turbulent flows are 105 times more efficient than molecular diffusion

Mechanical (forced) convection

  • Wind moving over a natural surface experiences skin friction as it drags along
    • Greater over rough surfaces → small eddies
  • Air moving at different speeds, or in different directions, shears over itself, causing overturning.

The production of forced convection requires a continual supply of kinetic energy from the flow. This comes from the mean wind speed which is driven by horizontal pressure differences which in turn are due to temperature differences at larger scales

Reynolds decomposition

All measured signals of turbulent properties appear chaotic – but can be analyzed by deconstructing them into two parts: the time mean (given an overbar), and the instantaneous deviation from the mean (given a prime), this is called Reynolds’ notation:

See fluctuations as eddies move by.

The same fluid can flow laminar or turbulent.

Eddies are coherent parts of the flow that show homogeneous properties. Mechanical and thermal convection create eddies (turbulence). We can use Reynold’s notation to separate a signal into a turbulent and a mean part.
