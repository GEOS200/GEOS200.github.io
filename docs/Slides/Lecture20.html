Airflow around a solid barrier

  • Three zones can be identified :
  • Displacement zone: the air is displaced upward and accelerated as it moves over the barrier.
  • Cavity (quiet) zone: mean wind speed is low and the effect of the turbulent wake flow from the top of the barrier is small.
  • Wake zone: mean wind is reduced and
  • turbulence is increased

As oncoming flow (A) approaches a building the mean flow streamlines are displaced, due to the build-up of a positive pressure perturbation on the windward face (see displacement zone B, Figure 4.4). Air diverges vertically and laterally to flow over and around the building. The flow diverges in all directions from this point: up over the roof, around the sides and down the front. negative pressure perturbation on the roof, side walls and leeward face of the building which create suction and induce air to circulate in the reverse direction to the main flow. Some of the air deflects down the face of the building to the ground. There it streams away from the face and back towards the approach flow, undercutting it and causing it to separate from the ground upwind and forms a standing eddy vortex near the base. Behind the building the flow structure is complex; flow separation dominates, as it streams past the rear edges of the side walls and roof leaving a suction zone in the lee of the building. This is the cavity zone C, which is part of the overall wake zone D, of the building (Figure 4.4). Immediately behind the corner of a building the lower surface pressure causes flow to recirculate against the approach flow creating a a vortex.. In the wake mean wind is reduced & turbulence is increased. Momentum – force the wind exerts on crops and trees. This is force per unit ground area is called shear stress (τ, N m-2). 

Upwind of the building (I), the profile is in equilibrium with the underlying surface. At location (II) the profile responds by accelerating, due to the confluence of the flow in the displacement zone, as it ‘squeezes’ over the top of the building.  Closer to the roof surface, in the cavity zone, there is a sharp decrease of speed in the zone of lower pressure above the roof, and counter-flow, especially immediately adjacent to its windward edge. Behind the building (III), the wind profile shows return flow and a marked velocity deficit (shelter) in the cavity. At location (IV), flow starts to restore the wind profile to its undisturbed state, however the velocity deficit persists for some distance (x), decaying at a rate of about x-1/5 and fading out by about x = 15H.

Wind breaks and snow drifting

Wind breaks with a density of 50% trap more snow than those with higher densities and are used to protect roads. Solid fences generate strong lee eddies behind and reduce snow trapping ability.

By doing this, it allows snowbanks to build upon the leeward side of the fence. In other words, if the wind is moving from left to right, a snow fence will cause snowbanks to develop on the right (not left) side of the fence.

Wind damage

Good reference: Windthrow Handbook for BC Forests. R.J Stathers, T.P Rollerson and S.J Mitchell. Working Paper 9401. Research Branch, BCMoF, Victoria

Wind damage at Prospect Point, Stanley Park 2006 ‘branch lines’, 18

Wind damage to plants includes lodging of agricultural crops and windthrow of trees. Wind damage causes an economic loss of more than $10 M/yr to the forest industry in B.C., ~100 ha lost/yr

Lodging occurs when the crop falls over and does not return to a standing position.  windthrow refers to trees uprooted by wind.

Wind throw management

  • 2 types:
  • Catastrophic
  • infrequent; exceptionally strong winds
  • Endemic
  • regular; smaller scale
  • result of numerous low wind speed storms
  • often due to poor forest management; can be reduced

Factors affecting wind damage

  • Individual tree character: crown size; presence of roots and bole root, stem taper.
  • Stand character: younger stands are more wind firm; susceptible in first years after harvest; low thinning intensity (<25%) is best.
  • Soil character: deep, well-drained – best; shallow, wet - bad.
  • Topographic site: valleys and saddles cause funneling of wind; mountain ridges – accelerate on windward side and eddies on the lee side.
  • Meteorological conditions: wind speed, gustiness and storm duration; soil moisture conditions; snow and freezing rain loading; few trees can withstand winds exceeding 30 m s-1 (100 km h-1) for more than 10 min.

  • Wind damage occurs when the wind load (i.e. the total drag force) on a plant exceeds the ability of the plant’s stem or root system to withstand the bending force, or torque. Mechanics of windthrow

Mechanics of windthrow

  • For swaying tree there are 2 components:
  • Fh – due to wind Fh = ½ CD A ρa u2
  • where CD – drag coefficient; A – silhouette area of tree; ρa – air density; u – wind speed (assumes steady wind)
  • Fg – due to gravity Fg = m g x
  • where m – mass of tree; x – amount of sway; g – force of gravity

Where does windthrow occur?

  • Windthrow usually happens at the boundaries of forests facing storm wind.

Where does windthrow occur?

Topography also induces windthrow. It occurs near the top of the windward slope where flow accelerates, and on the lee slope where turbulent eddies enhance the swaying of the tree tops.

Windthrow management strategies

    1. Avoid putting downwind boundaries (windward) in overly thinned stands, water saturated soils, swamps, diseased stands, shallow soil. Locate them where trees are wind firm.

Cutblocks - A specific area of land with defined boundaries, authorized for harvest.

Edge feathering—The act of creating a gradual transition between two habitat types. It is accomplished by cutting existing vegetation, such as timber, and/or by planting shrubs and grasses of varying heights.

Take home points

  • Wind breaks lower wind and turbulence over the crop. Medium density, porous wind breaks are most effective.
  • Winds can damage stands - some damage can be avoided by intelligent management of forests.
