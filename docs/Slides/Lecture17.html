The atmospheric boundary layer

The diurnal cycle of daytime heating and nocturnal cooling does not affect the whole atmosphere of Earth but is rather limited to a shallow layer (1 - 3 km during day, 100 - 500 m during night). This layer is called atmospheric boundary layer (ABL).

The atmospheric boundary layer

  • The atmospheric boundary layer reacts within several hours (< 1 day) to changes at the surface. Therefore we measure a diurnal cycle of temperature, humidity, wind and air pollutants in the ABL.
  • The ABL consists of: the surface layer (lowest 100 m or so), the mixed layer, and a “lid” of stable air.
  • Turbulence is best developed near the ground.

Local winds

(Photo: A. Christen)

  • Local-scale thermal winds are induced by horizontal pressure gradients in the ABL, due to differences in surface heating or cooling.
  • There is usually a circulation (thermal circulation) associated with these flows, i.e., air moving one way near the surface with an opposite (return) flow aloft.

Land and sea (lake) breeze system

  • By day - Tland > Tsea because water:
  • Allows sub-surface penetration of K↓ in a volume.
  • Allows convective mixing of heat in a volume.
  • Has greater evaporative use of Q*.
  • Has higher heat capacity hence warms less for unit Q*.
  • At night - Tland < Tsea because:
  • Higher heat capacity water cools more slowly.
  • Continued mixing of heat through deep layer of water but conduction only draws heat from shallow land layer.

Local winds at slopes

  • By day, hot air at heated slope surfaces is less dense than air at the same horizontal level over the valley – it rises:
  • → anabatic (upslope) breezes
  • At night, radiation cooling of slope surface makes air denser than at same horizontal level over valley – air sinks:
  • → katabatic (drainage) winds

Katabatic flows

The katabatic flows at night due to gravity, cause cold air to accumulate in low-lying areas or on the up-slope side of barriers (walls, hedges, embankments). Such pooling of cold air gives greatest frost risk at low spots. Mid-way up valley sides are the mildest – called the thermal belt.

Local winds in main mountain valleys

By day, in mountainous areas, the air at higher elevation warms more rapidly than that down the valley triggering up-slope (anabatic) flow and also up-valley flow.

T.R. Oke (1987): ‘Boundary Layer Climates’

At night the rapidly cooling air on mountains flows down by gravity (katabatic flows) and combines to form mountain winds flowing down the main valley

Mountain and valley winds

  • These winds have been observed in a 1 km deep valley on Mt. Rainier, Washington (Buettner, 1967)
  • The existence of such regular patterns have implications for the spread of air pollutants, seeds, pathogens etc.

Regional winds

There are also many regional winds with special names. Many have meso- or synoptic scale roots but are essentially drainage winds (Squamish, Howler, Bora, Mistral) or due to adiabatic compression heating (Chinook, Santa Ana, Föhn)

Take home points

  • Atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) - the layer where we measure a diurnal course of temperature, humidity, pollutants etc.
  • Local winds are induced by horizontal pressure gradients in the ABL, due to differences in surface heating or cooling.
  • Common local wind systems reverse between day and night and are: land and sea breeze, valley winds, slope winds. Daytime upward winds are called anabatic, nocturnal downslope winds are called katabatic winds.
