Photosynthesis 

The formation of carbohydrates from water and carbon dioxide in the chlorophyll-containing tissues of plants exposed to light or photosynthetically active radiation, PAR (400-700 nm). 

Respiration  The oxidation of carbohydrates with the release of CO2 and energy, occurs continuously to provide energy for other plant processes. 

The difference between photosynthesis and respiration in the leaf is referred to as net photosynthesis or net assimilation. 

Photosynthesis and respiration in the leaf

Photosynthesis and cellular respiration are like two sides of the same coin. The products of one process are the reactants of the other. Growth respiration (to grow new tissue, repair injured tissue) and maintenance respiration (re-synthesis of compounds, maintenance of chemical gradients, metabolic processes in physiological adjustment to change in plant’s environment). Together, the two processes store and release energy in living organisms. The two processes also work together to recycle oxygen in Earth’s atmosphere.

Photosynthetic efficiency of the leaf

Increasing photon flux above this point results in a proportional increase in photosynthetic rate, represented by a linear relationship between photon flux and photosynthetic rate. In this part photosynthesis is light limited, so the more light is incident the more photosynthesis comes about. Thus, the plant reaches a “light saturation point” and the rate of photosynthesis is limited due to a limited amount of carbon dioxide, or due to some other limiting factor (e.g. temperature).

At higher photon fluxes, the photosynthetic response to light starts to level off and reaches saturation, after which further increase in photon flux no longer affect photosynthetic rates, indicating that factors other than incident light, (electron transport rate, rubisco activity etc.) limit photosynthesis. After the saturation point, photosynthesis is referred to as CO2 limited, due to the inability of the Calvin cycle enzymes to keep pace with the absorbed light energy.

Net photosynthetic rates in agricultural crops and forests 

  • Most data indicate that ordinary crops/forests convert less than 1% of the incident solar radiation over the growing season. 
  • Under optimum conditions, this might reach 2.5% for the growing season and 4% over the period of a day in the middle of the growing season of a corn crop.

Ecosystem respiration (Re)

  • Re is made up of plant respiration and that of microbes that decompose soil organic matter and detritus (dead leaves (litterfall), rotten logs, stumps, etc.). 
  • The former is referred to as autotrophic respiration (Ra) & goes on in leaves (as we’ve mentioned), for growth and maintenance of branches, stems, & roots. The latter is referred to as heterotrophic respiration (Rh). We can write:
  • Re = Ra + Rh 
  • The amount of carbon an ecosystem sequesters depends on not only plant respiration but also the rate of organic matter decomposition.
  • Both are strongly affected by climate (temperature) and soil conditions (aeration, soil moisture).

Autotrophic & heterotrophic soil respiration

  • Rs is composed of the CO2 produced by microbial decomposition of soil organic matter (SOM) (heterotrophic respiration, Rsh) and by root respiration (autotrophic respiration, Rsa)
  • Rs is also strongly affected by climate (temperature) and soil conditions (aeration, soil moisture). 

Relationship between Rs and temperature 

Rs increases exponentially with T, which is often expressed as:

Q10

  • Q10 is the relative increase in Rs (or Re) for a 10 K increase in T
  • Q10 is about 2 in many biological processes but is frequently found to be as much as 3 for Re and Rs.
  • As an example of using the equation below, assuming Q10 = 2 and Tref = 10 °C, when T = 20 °C, Rs = Rref2[(20-10)/10] = 2 Rref.

Will ecosystems continue to help offset CO2 emissions?

We know that these different components of the C cycle are changing over time. FF increasing &LUC Both ocean and land CO2 sinks increased since the 1960s, roughly in line with the atmospheric increase, While at the moment clearly ecosystems are helping us deal with atmospheric CO2, Any significant decrease in these carbon sinks means that we’re going to have to work harder in the future from keeping GHGs from accumulating in the atmosphere. And the increased uptake in CO2 from the land is thought to be due to the combined effects of CO2 fertilization, N deposition, as well as the effects of climate change such as the lengthening of the growing season in northern temperate and boreal areas. However, we see significant variability in the land sink, particularly on decadal scales (decreased land sink during El Niño events) And there’s evidence suggesting that land and ocean are becoming steadily less effective at removing excess carbon dioxide from the atmosphere (due to intrinsic climate-carbon feedbacks) with important implications. This is of great importance to climate policymaking as it strongly influences compatible emissions for any climate stabilization target. And therefore studying land-surface interactions is critical for a better understanding of the land sink component and how it will change over time, but also for understanding how land use and land-use change will affect GHG emissions and how this affects the overall global carbon cycle

The increased storage in terrestrial ecosystems not affected by land use change is likely to be caused by enhanced photosynthesis at higher CO2 levels and nitrogen deposition, and changes in climate favoring carbon sinks such as longer growing seasons in mid-to-high latitudes.

Take home points

  • Photosynthesis is the combination of H2O and CO2 in leaves to form carbohydrates. They are oxidized in respiration. 
  • Photosynthetic efficiency decreases with increasing PAR. Plant canopy photosynthesis accounts for less than 1% of solar irradiance. 
  • Respiration is the sum of autotrophic (plant) and heterotrophic (organic matter decomposition) respiration. 
  • Respiration is exponentially related to temperature and therefore is likely to be sensitive to global warming. 
  • The net CO2 budget (NEE) is equal to Re-GEP
